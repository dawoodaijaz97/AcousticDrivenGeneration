# Summary of "Acoustic-Driven Generation of Pathological Speech Reports Using Large Language Models"

## Abstract
Speech disorders are seldom analyzed through standardized medical reports due to the lack of consistent protocols. 
The modified Frenchay Dysarthria Assessment (mFDA) scale is one of the few available tools for quantifying speech impairment across seven categories — breathing, lips, larynx, palate, monotonicity, tongue, and intelligibility. 
This study proposes using Large Language Models (LLMs) to automatically generate FDA-like clinical reports from audio recordings by leveraging acoustic biomarkers correlated with mFDA categories. 
The dataset includes speech recordings from 50 Parkinson’s disease (PD) patients and 50 healthy controls (HC). The proposed approach demonstrates that LLMs can effectively generate clinically interpretable reports, achieving BLEU scores of 0.789 for PD and 0.836 for HC, showing potential for practical clinical applications.

## Introduction
Speech is a multidimensional biosignal that reflects various aspects of human health. In Parkinson’s disease (PD), speech is often impaired due to dysarthria — a motor speech disorder caused by neurological dysfunction affecting muscle control during speech. Despite its diagnostic relevance, speech is rarely assessed in PD evaluations.  
The Frenchay Dysarthria Assessment (FDA-2) and its modified version (mFDA) enable clinicians to evaluate speech across categories like breathing, articulation, and intelligibility. However, such assessments are subjective, time-consuming, and dependent on expert interpretation.  
This paper addresses these challenges by combining acoustic analysis and LLMs to generate structured, interpretable clinical text reports from speech biomarkers.

## Problem Statement
While acoustic biomarkers can objectively describe speech characteristics, their interpretation requires expertise in signal processing. Conversely, perceptual scales like mFDA rely on subjective human evaluation. Bridging these two approaches is essential for scalable, interpretable, and automated clinical assessment.

## Proposed Approach
The study introduces a **speech-based framework** that uses acoustic biomarkers as input prompts to fine-tuned LLMs, enabling the automatic generation of mFDA-based text reports.  
Two main innovations are:
1. A speech-to-text report generation pipeline where LLMs receive acoustic biomarker inputs instead of textual prompts.  
2. A data augmentation strategy that simulates biomarker–report pairs to expand training data and improve model generalization.

## Related Work
Automatic Medical Report Generation (aMRG) is an emerging field, with prior work focusing on radiology, pathology, and ophthalmology. Earlier systems used spoken instructions within ASR frameworks, whereas recent approaches rely on multimodal and contrastive learning architectures such as CLIP, OpenFlamingo, and LLaMA.  
This paper is the **first to extend aMRG methods to pathological speech**, using audio signals as the *primary information source* for clinical report generation.

## Dataset
The study used the **PC-GITA dataset**, comprising speech samples from 50 PD and 50 HC Colombian Spanish speakers (balanced by gender).  
Each participant performed four speech tasks: sustained vowel /a/, rapid repetition of /pa-ta-ka/, reading a short text, and spontaneous conversation.  
Three expert phoniatricians rated the recordings using the mFDA scale (0–4). Median scores were mapped to categorical labels:  
- **0–1:** Normal  
- **2:** Mild  
- **3:** Moderate  
- **4:** Severe

## Acoustic Biomarkers
A total of **248 task-specific acoustic biomarkers** were extracted from the recordings, covering three main domains:

| Feature Group | Description | Related mFDA Categories | Speech Task |
|----------------|-------------|--------------------------|--------------|
| **Phonation** | Voice stability and loudness | Larynx | Sustained vowel /a/ |
| **Prosody** | Rhythm, intonation, timing | Monotonicity, Breathing | Reading & Conversation |
| **Phonemic** | Articulatory precision and coordination | Lips, Tongue, Palate, Intelligibility | /pa-ta-ka/ & Reading |

Feature selection was performed using **Kruskal–Wallis tests** and **point-biserial correlation**, identifying the seven most correlated biomarkers for each mFDA category.

## Language Models
Several pre-trained LLMs from Hugging Face were fine-tuned for this task:
- **T5** and **Flan-T5** (encoder-decoder, text-to-text)  
- **GPT-2** (decoder-only)  
- **LLaMA-7B**, **Mistral-7B**, and **DeepSeek-Qwen2.5-7B** (large transformer-based models with 7B parameters)

## Training and Data Augmentation
Given the small dataset, **synthetic biomarker–report pairs** were generated by sampling from the statistical distributions of real acoustic biomarkers.  
- **Total simulated pairs:** 110,000 (100K for training, 10K for validation)  
- **Real reports:** 100 (used exclusively for testing)  
Models were fine-tuned for 3 epochs on an NVIDIA A100 (40 GB), with a learning rate of 3e-4, weight decay of 1e-2, and batch size of 8.

## Results
Performance was evaluated using **ROUGE**, **BLEU**, and **BERT** metrics.  
- **Top-performing models:** LLaMA-7B, Mistral-7B, and DeepSeek-7B  
- **Best BLEU scores:** 0.789 (PD) and 0.836 (HC)  
- **Observation:** Models performed better on HC data due to fewer outliers in acoustic features.  
Smaller models (T5, GPT-2) showed lower accuracy and poorer report structure consistency.

## Conclusion
This work introduces a **novel acoustic-driven clinical report generation framework**, bridging quantitative speech analysis and qualitative medical interpretation.  
The approach improves interpretability and user control, demonstrating that **LLMs can associate acoustic biomarkers with clinical speech ratings** to generate structured, human-readable reports.  
Although large models (7B parameters) achieved the best results, the study highlights the feasibility of using speech-derived acoustic data to assist clinicians in assessing pathological speech non-invasively.
